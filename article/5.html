Github is a game I refuse to play

The current system is just anxiety inducing

I used to genuinely love Github but it’s become a popularity contest where no one wins

I was having a conversation with a friend who’s looking to break into the tech industry, and one of the really surprising things he said was that he was looking to “contribute” to open-source projects. I asked why he was doing that, and he said it’s “because recruiters love to see you contributing to open source.” [1]

While I acknowledge that doing open-source work could be a great experience for someone by showing them how software is built, this is frankly a sad state of affairs.

In the past, developers would contribute to software because they loved their craft and loved the application/tool/system they were contributing to. This was their way of saying “Thank you.” This was their way of being part of a community.

This is also my way of saying “Thank you.” It’s what got me into open source as well. It means so much to me that the people who maintain my favorite software receive some kind of help that, whenever I had a chance, I genuinely try to assist. I am by no means prolific, nor am I claiming to be some open source deity, but at least I know it comes from a good place.

Under pressure

I have a full-time software job now. I’ve had one for a couple of years now and my green patches have become more akin to a desert with a few sparse grass strands, but they’re still there. I still use open source technology, and I still encounter bugs that make me feel like I could fix them when I have free time. Sometimes, it takes me so long to get around to fixing them that it appears as if I forgot halfway, when the truth is I simply haven’t had the time.

When I look at my commit history, I can’t help but feel like it’s telling the world that I’m not doing enough, that I’m not working fast enough, or that I don’t care enough. [2]

I do care; I just have other things going on.

Awful contributions for clout

The current gamified model means that open source maintainers are getting inundated with low-quality PRs from people looking to say they “contributed” to big projects like React, Vite, or Tensorflow.

This only adds extra stress to the maintainers who have to go through the commits. By going private, there’s zero incentive to put a PR in other than “this is legitimately a real issue I want to solve here.” It removes the ego from the equation.

A terrible metric begging to be gamed

I once was reviewing a resume and I saw that one of the candidates had a profile full of green. Too green - like painting a square a certain shade. Turned out the guy had a bot that was just pumping a commit into some repo. That’s where we are right now.

A note on whether doing this will hurt my chances of getting hired

I have a pretty active profile

You might think: Wow! This guy must be coding all the time! Truth is a lot of these are come from writing my own apps, updating the blog or just doing little bits here or there. A glance at this or my activity isn’t an indicator of my quality - a commit is more than just a commit to a big name open source project. And Github is more than just a code sharing website now.

On going dark

What I am doing is right for me.

My relationship with FOSS remains authentic and that removes a lot of the mental baggage of trying to have a “perfect” Github profile.

I refuse to play that game, and the quality of my work is better for it.
